Bookkeeping tools

What software do you use to track sales and expenses?

The right tracking tool is the one that captures every sale and receipt without adding extra work.

The Narrative (Left Column)

The Empathy

You have numbers scattered everywhere: a card reader dashboard, a cash notebook, and a stack of receipts in a tote. When you sit down to reconcile it all, the totals don't line up and you're left guessing what really happened at the market. You want a system that fits your booth days, not a system that demands hours of cleanup after every event.

The Education

Tracking tools fall into two buckets: spreadsheets and dedicated bookkeeping software. Spreadsheets are free or very low cost, flexible, and easy to start, but they rely on manual entry and consistent formulas. One missed line or a broken formula can throw off your totals. Bookkeeping software automates categorization, imports bank transactions, and stores receipts. That automation reduces errors and makes tax time easier, but it comes with a monthly subscription and a short learning curve.

Think about your volume and time: if you only sell a few times per month, a well-structured spreadsheet might be enough. If you sell weekly, take card payments, and have lots of receipts, software can save hours and provide real-time profit snapshots you can trust.

The Solution

Compare the options by cost and effort, then pick the simplest system you'll keep using.

  • Spreadsheets: Typically free with tools like Google Sheets or a one-time template purchase. Best for low volume, but requires disciplined manual entry and regular checks.
  • Bookkeeping software: Subscription-based, often in the range of $10–$30 per month for solo vendors. Good for automation, receipt storage, and easy reporting.
  • Payment processor reports: Often included with card readers, useful for sales totals but usually not enough for tracking expenses or cash income.

If you start with a spreadsheet, schedule a weekly 15-minute update. If you move to software, connect your bank and set simple categories (materials, booth fees, packaging, travel) so reports stay clean. The best choice is the one that keeps your numbers accurate without taking you away from the booth.
