Cash control

How do you keep track of cash sales?

Cash slips through the cracks unless you build a simple routine for logging it.

The Narrative (Left Column)

The Empathy

Cash is fast and convenient, but it's also the easiest to lose track of. Between making change and answering questions, it's common to forget a sale or mix business cash with personal bills. That uncertainty makes it hard to trust your end-of-day totals.

The Education

Cash tracking works best when you separate the cash float (starting change) from sales, then reconcile at the end of the event. If you start with $100 in change and end with $380 in the box, you made $280 in cash sales. Logging cash sales daily creates a consistent record that matches deposits and taxes.

The Solution

Use a simple cash log and reconciliation routine each event.

  • Set a fixed starting cash float and write it down before the event starts.
  • Record each cash sale in a tally sheet or a quick POS cash button during the day.
  • At close, count cash, subtract the starting float, and log the cash sales total.

Deposit sales regularly and keep the cash log with your receipts so your numbers stay audit-proof.

How cash sales show up for taxes

Cash is still taxable income, so the goal is to create a paper trail that matches deposits and your tax records. When your cash log reconciles to your bank deposits and sales totals, you can confidently report the numbers even if you never received a 1099 form.

  • Keep daily cash totals alongside card sales so you have a full income record.
  • Deposit cash consistently instead of letting it mix with personal spending.
  • Store event notes or receipts with the cash log for backup documentation.

Do you record each sale or total it up?

Either method can work, but consistency matters more than detail. If your volume is high, tallying total cash at the end of the event is usually enough as long as you reconcile it with the starting float and POS totals.

  • For low-volume shows, jot each cash sale to help spot mistakes.
  • For busy shows, use a quick tally sheet and log a single total at close.
  • Always reconcile cash to your recorded sales before depositing.

What to do with the cash after a fair

Treat the cash like any other business income. Deposit it into your business account quickly and keep the change float separate so you don’t spend sales money by accident.

  • Reset the change fund to its starting amount and store it for the next event.
  • Deposit the remaining cash within a day or two to keep records clean.
  • Match the deposit to your cash log so reports stay accurate.
