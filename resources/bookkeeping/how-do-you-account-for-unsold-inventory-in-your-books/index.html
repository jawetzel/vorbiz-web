Inventory accounting

How do you account for unsold inventory in your books?

Unsold inventory is still an asset, not a loss, until it leaves your shelf.

The Narrative (Left Column)

The Empathy

You finish a show with bins still full and wonder how to reflect that in your numbers. It can feel like a loss, but those items still have value and can sell at the next event.

The Education

Inventory accounting separates what you made from what you sold. The costs of materials move into inventory and only become “cost of goods sold” when the item sells. Keeping a simple count of starting stock, sales, and ending stock lets you see the true profit for each period.

The Solution

Track inventory as an asset and only expense it when it sells.

  • Record materials or finished goods as inventory until sold.
  • Subtract sold quantities from inventory to calculate cost of goods sold.
  • Review unsold stock quarterly to spot slow movers or overproduction.

This approach keeps your profit numbers accurate and helps you plan smarter production runs.

How unsold inventory affects profitability

Unsold stock ties up cash, but it doesn't count as a loss until it is written off or sold at a discount. The financial impact shows up when you compare the cash spent on materials to the revenue generated at each show.

  • Track slow movers so you can discount or bundle them strategically.
  • Review inventory levels before restocking to avoid cash flow strain.
  • Separate markdowns from full-price sales to see real profit trends.
