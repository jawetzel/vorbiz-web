Is having a clearance or sale bin at your table a good idea?

A clearance bin can move slow inventory, but it can also cheapen the perception of your brand.

The Narrative

The Empathy

You're staring at boxes of items that just won't move and wondering if a sale bin is the answer. It feels like a quick way to clear space and recover some costs, but you also worry it could train shoppers to wait for a discount or make your main pricing look inflated.

The Education

Clearance bins work best when they are intentional and limited. They are useful for seasonal items, older styles, or inventory that costs you storage and time. The risk is brand dilution: if the sale bin is front and center or looks messy, it can signal that everything is negotiable. A balanced approach is to keep the bin separate from your primary display, label it clearly as "last chance" or "retired styles," and ensure the markdown still covers your minimum costs. Think of it as an inventory management tool, not a primary sales strategy.

The Solution

Use a small, cleanly labeled clearance bin at the edge of the booth and limit it to 10–15% of your inventory. Set a floor price that protects your materials cost, and restock it only after a season or product refresh. Pair it with signage that reinforces value, like "retired designs—limited quantities." This keeps the bin from undermining your brand while still turning slow movers into cash.
