Pricing guidance

Do you ever raise prices on an item that’s selling out?

Sellouts are a signal that demand is stronger than your current pricing or production capacity.

The Narrative

The Empathy

You keep selling out of the same item, and it's both exciting and stressful. You wonder if the price is too low, but you don't want to surprise loyal customers or look like you are gouging. At the same time, the rush to restock can burn you out.

The Education

Repeated sellouts mean demand is outpacing supply. A modest, planned price increase can help balance that and protect your time. Start by checking your margins—if you are already at your target, a small increase (5–10%) is usually enough to slow demand without killing it. You can also raise prices when costs rise or when you improve the product. Communicate the change in a positive way: "New batch, updated materials" or "limited run" signals that the item is evolving, not just getting pricier.

The Solution

Track sell-through by show and set a threshold, like selling out in the first half of two consecutive markets. When you hit that mark, test a small price increase for the next batch and watch the conversion rate. Keep the adjustment consistent across channels and update signage at the same time. A measured increase helps keep your workload sustainable while still rewarding demand.
