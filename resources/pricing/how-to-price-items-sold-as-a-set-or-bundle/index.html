How to price items sold as a set or bundle?

Bundles should feel like a deal for customers while still protecting your margin and labor.

The Narrative

The Empathy

You watch shoppers pick up two items, hesitate at the total, and set one back down. You know the set looks better together, but you also know you cannot afford to slash prices every time someone wants more than one piece. The pressure to create a "bundle deal" can feel like a tug-of-war between value for customers and stability for your business.

The Education

Bundle pricing works best when you start with the full individual prices, then offer a small, intentional incentive that is funded by real savings (like shared packaging, fewer transactions, or planned overstock). Common strategies include tiered savings (buy two, save 10%), anchor bundles (pair a hero item with an add-on), and curated sets (three coordinated items at a fixed price). For example, if your individual prices are $28, $28, and $18, a curated set at $65 gives the customer a clear savings while still exceeding your target margin. Another option is a "complete kit" bundle: a $40 candle plus a $12 wick trimmer for $48, which feels like a win but keeps the premium item leading the price.

The Solution

Build bundles from your margin sheet, not from impulse. Decide the maximum discount you can afford (often 5–15%), then apply it consistently. Create signage that highlights both the individual value and the bundle price: "Set of three for $65 (save $9)." Track bundle sell-through so you can swap in different add-ons or adjust the savings without undercutting your core pricing. When bundles are intentional, they lift average order value instead of eroding your profits.
