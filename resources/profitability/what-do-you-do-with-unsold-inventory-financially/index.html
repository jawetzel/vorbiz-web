Profitability question

What do you do with unsold inventory financially?

Unsold inventory isn't lost money yet, but it does tie up cash, so you need to decide whether to carry it forward at full cost or write it down to reality.

The Narrative (Left Column)

The Empathy

The market is over, the bins are back in the car, and you're staring at the table of items that didn't sell. It's hard not to feel like those pieces are a personal failure, especially when the supply receipts are still fresh in your wallet. You can see the cash from today's sales, but you also feel the weight of everything that came home with you. That tension makes it tough to know if the day was profitable or just expensive.

The Education

Financially, unsold inventory lives in two paths. The first is inventory carryover: you keep the remaining items on your books at their cost, meaning the money you spent is still sitting in an asset, not an expense. The second is a write-down: if the items are damaged, stale, or realistically need a deep discount, you reduce their book value and record the loss now. Carryover keeps profit higher on paper, while a write-down reduces profit to match what the inventory can truly earn.

Cash flow is the other half of the story. The cash already left your account when you bought supplies, so unsold inventory represents cash that's tied up and unavailable for restocking or paying bills. A slow-moving pile of inventory can choke cash flow even if your profit report looks fine. The sooner you decide whether to carry, discount, or clear it out, the faster you can turn that locked-up cash into spendable funds again.

The Solution

Build a simple routine that tracks quantity, cost, and age for each product. If something consistently sells, carry it forward at cost and keep your margin expectations intact. If it sits too long, choose a planned markdown, bundle it with a bestseller, or donate it and record the write-down so your books reflect reality. A clear inventory system makes it easy to protect profit while also managing the cash flow that keeps your booth running week after week.
