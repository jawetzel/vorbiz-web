Profitability question

Do you count your labor time when calculating profit?

If your profit ignores the hours you put in, you're missing the real cost of running your booth.

The Narrative (Left Column)

The Empathy

You wrap up a long market day, count the cash, and feel good because the sales number looks strong. Then you remember the prep time: the hours spent making inventory, packing the car, and setting up the booth before the first customer even arrived. It's easy to treat that time as "free" because you didn't write a check for it, but it still came out of your schedule and energy.

The Education

Labor valuation is the missing ingredient in real profit. Your time has a value, even if you're the owner. Think in opportunity cost: what could you earn or do instead with those hours? For example, if a show nets $180 after supplies and fees, but you spent 6 hours prepping and selling, and your time is worth $20 per hour, that's $120 of labor cost. The true profit is $180 minus $120, or $60. When you include labor, you can compare events by profit per hour instead of just sales totals.

The Solution

Build a simple habit: log your event hours alongside sales and expenses. Assign a baseline hourly rate and subtract it just like you would a booth fee. With that view, you can spot which markets actually pay you what you're worth, raise prices on low-margin items, and decide when to say no to time-heavy events. A clear system turns labor from a guess into a confident yes-or-no filter.
