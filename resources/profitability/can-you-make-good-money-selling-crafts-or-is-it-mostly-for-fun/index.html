Profitability question

Can you make good money selling crafts, or is it mostly for fun?

It can be real income, but most crafters earn modest profits per event until they dial in pricing, costs, and repeat sales.

The Narrative (Left Column)

The Empathy

It's common to end a craft fair with a full cash box and still wonder if it was worth the late nights. You remember the booth fee, the gas, the snacks, and the hours spent prepping inventory, but none of it is totaled in one place. One weekend feels like a win, the next feels like a wash, and it's hard to tell if you're running a business or just funding a hobby you love.

The Education

Craft income lives on a wide spectrum. A hobby seller doing one or two markets a month might average $300-$700 in sales, spend $120-$300 on materials and packaging, pay a $40-$80 booth fee, and net $80-$250 for a 6-10 hour day (roughly $10-$25 per hour). A stronger event with premium pricing could look like $1,200 in sales with 40% costs, a $100 fee, and $520 left over for eight hours of work ($65 per hour). On the flip side, a slow rainy day might bring $200 in sales with $180 in costs, leaving just $20. The swing depends on booth fees, product mix, price points, production time, payment fees, travel distance, seasonality, and whether you also drive repeat sales online.

The Solution

The answer is a tracking routine that shows the real hourly profit for each event. Log sales, every cost, and the total hours so you can compare shows, set a minimum sales target, and price for the margins you need. Use the data to build bundles, push higher-margin items, and plan follow-up sales (custom orders, online restocks, or wholesale) that turn one market day into a longer earning window. Once you can see the numbers, you can decide whether it's mostly for fun, a steady side hustle, or something you're ready to scale.
